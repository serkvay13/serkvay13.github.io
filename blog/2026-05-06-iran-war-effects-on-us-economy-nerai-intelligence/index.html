Executive Summary

An armed conflict between the United States and Iran, now underway as of May 2026, presents a significant macroeconomic challenge to the U.S. economy through multiple transmission mechanisms: energy market disruption, fiscal strain from military expenditures, inflationary pressures on consumer prices, and heightened financial market volatility.

While the U.S. economy's post-industrial structure and reduced manufacturing base provide some insulation from the most severe oil-shock consequences, the current U.S. baseline — characterized by elevated debt levels (123% debt-to-GDP), tight labor markets, and fragile consumer confidence — creates conditions under which even moderate war-related shocks carry outsized macroeconomic costs.

NERAI analysis finds that the Iran war poses a material downside risk to near-term GDP growth, with inflation and interest-rate dynamics presenting the primary transmission channels for economic damage. The NERAI Iran Deteriorating Bilateral Relations index at 0.0543 signals approximately 40–50% elevated probability of conflict expansion over the 12-month horizon.

Key Findings

Energy Markets

Disruption to Iranian oil exports (3.8 mbd, ~3.8% of global supply) and regional shipping lanes creates upward crude price pressure. U.S. shale (13 mbd domestic) and SPR access (370M barrels) provide partial hedges, but 3–6 month mobilization lags limit near-term relief.

Fiscal & Monetary Constraint

Military supplementals of $50–250B compound an already 5.5% structural deficit. Federal Reserve faces inflation-recession trade-off with policy rates at 4.5–4.75% — substantially above the 2% estimated neutral rate — constraining conventional responses.

Consumer Demand Vulnerability

Consumer spending represents 68% of U.S. GDP. War-driven equity corrections (3–5% in early May 2026) reduce household net worth by $15,000–30,000 for median investors. Q2–Q3 consumption growth decelerates to 0.8–1.2% annualized in the base case.

Financial Market Repricing

Treasury yield volatility, equity corrections, and credit spread widening observed in early May 2026 reflect investor reassessment of growth and inflation trajectories — a process that amplifies economic contraction through wealth effects and tighter lending conditions.

Economic Transmission Channels

1

Energy Prices → Inflation

Every $10/barrel crude increase translates to approximately 0.3 percentage points of additional U.S. inflation within 12 months (CEPR, 3 May 2026). At the base-case $90–110/barrel range, this implies 0.8–1.3pp inflationary impulse on top of the existing 3.9% rate — pushing year-end 2026 inflation to 4.8–5.2%.

2

Military Expenditures → Fiscal Deterioration

Supplemental appropriations of $50–250B compound an already 5.5% structural deficit, increasing Treasury issuance and upward pressure on long-term rates in an environment where the Fed holds policy rates at 4.5–4.75%. Higher yields then feed through to mortgage, auto loan, and corporate borrowing costs.

3

Equity Corrections → Wealth Effects → Consumption

S&P 500 declined 3–5% in early May 2026. For the median household with 60% of retirement savings in equities, a 5–10% correction equals $15,000–30,000 in lost wealth. Consumer confidence deterioration typically precedes actual spending cuts by 1–2 quarters, signaling Q2–Q3 2026 deceleration.

4

Fed Policy Dilemma → Investment Paralysis

The Federal Reserve faces an unresolvable short-term tension: inflation demands restrictive rates, but demand destruction warrants accommodation. This policy uncertainty itself suppresses business investment and hiring. Firms delay capital expenditure decisions amid unclear rate trajectories — amplifying the demand-destruction channel.

Policy Recommendations

1

SPR Management & Domestic Supply Incentives

Authorize SPR drawdowns of 50–70 million barrels to maintain crude in the $80–95/bbl band through Q3 2026. Simultaneously implement temporary permitting acceleration and investment tax credits for shale producers. This dual approach moderates inflation transmission while establishing longer-term supply buffers.

2

Temporary Fiscal Offset Mechanisms

Rather than pure deficit financing of war supplementals, implement a temporary 2% "conflict surtax" on corporate profits and incomes above $500,000 to offset 40–60% of military appropriations. This preserves fiscal credibility in capital markets and moderates long-term Treasury yield impacts at a 123% debt-to-GDP ratio.

3

Federal Reserve Two-Pillar Forward Guidance

The Fed should articulate an explicit framework: inflation-fighting commitment maintained while pre-committing to accommodation if 5-year-forward inflation expectations stay below 2.5%. This resolves the policy uncertainty that currently amplifies financial market volatility beyond what the underlying conflict shock warrants.

4

Targeted Household Support ($500–800 transfers)

Direct fiscal transfers to households below 150% of median income, funded through temporary payroll tax credits, timed to Q2–Q3 2026 when inflation transmission peaks. Energy inflation disproportionately affects lower-income households spending 12–15% of income on energy — transfers support aggregate demand while distributing conflict costs more progressively.

5

Diplomatic Off-Ramp with Economic Credibility

Signal to China, India, Russia, and EU that U.S. commitment to conflict limitation creates shared economic interests in rapid resolution. Establish transparent Iran communication channels with sanctions relief tied to specific conflict limitation agreements — creating an incentive structure for negotiated settlement and directly reducing the NERAI bilateral relations index tail-risk.
