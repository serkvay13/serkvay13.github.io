The diagnostic inversion. In systems undergoing genuine cascade-phase instability (Egypt 2011–13, Libya 2011, Thailand 2014), coup indices spike and persist or accelerate; forecasted probabilities do not decline. The NERAI Russia pattern is the inverse: near-term spike combined with a 36% projected decline in 12-month coup probability. This is the signature of acute threat-perception adjustment followed by institutional counter-mobilisation — not regime decay.

Analytical Framework — Securitization as Stabilization

This brief assesses Russia's post-disclosure institutional trajectory through the lens of securitization-as-stabilization: the hypothesis that heightened regime-threat perception, when successfully channeled into visible institutional response (purges, security expansion, military resource reallocation), can paradoxically reinforce elite coordination around succession protocols rather than triggering cascading defection.

The evidence for this framework operates at three levels:

  • NERAI index structure — rising near-term threat perception concurrent with declining 12-month destabilisation probability is the diagnostic signature of managed threat response.
  • Historical pattern — Putin's response to elite-fragmentation crises (2012–14 Bolotnaya wave, 2016–17 oligarch-prosecution wave) followed the same securitisation → institutional response → elite re-stabilisation arc.
  • Academic consensus — Henry E. Hale's elite-coordination framework, Tatiana Stanovaya's siloviki-faction dynamics, and Mark Galeotti's institutional-competition analysis converge on the assessment that Russian autocratic stability depends not on coercive capacity but on elite credibility in succession management — credibility that increases when the leader demonstrates capacity for institutional response to threat signals.

Three Forward Scenarios (12-Month Horizon, May 2026 → May 2027)

Scenario 1 — Managed Securitization (Base Case)

Analytical Likelihood: ~35%

Putin consolidates internal elite coordination around a war-extended, externally-securitised equilibrium. Recent removals are concluded as managed elite repositioning; FSO expansion is formalised and incorporated into factional resource-distribution agreements. Military spending stays at 5.5–6.0% of GDP. Internal factional competition continues but remains administrative and contained within security-service channels. No cascade-phase political crisis. NERAI projection: Coup Index 48–52, Govt Instability 2.5–4.0, Political Instability 0.050–0.070.

Scenario 2 — Constrained Escalation (Stress Case)

Analytical Likelihood: ~45%

The disclosure crystallises elite doubt more deeply than the Kremlin response can address. Factional competition accelerates rather than stabilises. Purges expand beyond mid-level FSO/GRU personnel to higher-ranking commanders or oligarch-linked officials. A secondary cascading event (military setback, sanctions shock, leaked elite communication) occurs by Q3/Q4 2026. Succession speculation becomes semi-public through faction-aligned media. No regime collapse, but severe institutional strain. NERAI projection: Coup Index 55–70, Govt Instability 5.0–8.0, Military Escalation 0.220–0.250 (accelerating).

Scenario 3 — Rapid Consolidation (Upside Case)

Analytical Likelihood: ~20%

Putin executes a decisive institutional reset by late Q2 2026. High-profile factional leaders perceived as succession threats are prosecuted or exiled on corruption/security charges. Succession protocol (likely involving a trusted technocrat) is formalised and communicated to elite. By Q3 2026 elite coordination has hardened decisively; coup indices decline sharply. Succession risk is managed, not eliminated — but it becomes predictable. NERAI projection: Coup Index 38–48, Govt Instability 1.5–2.5, Political Instability 0.040–0.060.

The Bilateral Asymmetry — External Securitization Substituting for Internal Settlement

A non-obvious but decisive insight emerges from the bilateral US-Russia indices. Russia's deteriorating-relations score stands at 48.1 while the US deteriorating-relations score is only 18.8 — a 29.3-point asymmetric gap. Russia perceives threat escalation from the United States at roughly 2.5× the rate the United States perceives threat from Russia.

This perception gap is functional, not random. When internal elite cohesion is fragmented, external threat perception can serve as a unity-mechanism: elites coordinate against external threat because doing so provides cover for internal repositioning, and external military mobilisation generates resource flows that can be distributed to factional allies. The May 2026 environment mirrors the 2012–14 Bolotnaya-then-Ukraine arc: external threat perception is elevated relative to internal threat perception on the US side, creating opportunity for Putin-aligned factions to consolidate internally while claiming external-securitisation rationale. Counter-Thesis — The Autonomous Regime Collapse Interpretation

The strongest opposing view comes from CFR's Andrea Kendall-Taylor (2024–2026 CFR Russia/Eurasia work), who has argued that Ukraine war attrition, Western sanctions on oligarchs and banks, and demographic turnover within the security elite have exhausted the traditional mechanisms by which Putin managed elite coordination — factional bribery through resource-distribution, external securitisation through military adventure, and selective coercion against rival factions. In this framework, the recent disclosure is not a symptom of managed fragmentation but a trigger for cascade-phase breakdown.

A distinctly more nuanced position is articulated by Maria Snegovaya (CSIS Russia Program; Carnegie Endowment 2022, "Has War Made Putin a Burden for Russia's Elites?"; Journal of Democracy 2023, "Will Putin Outlast the War?"). Snegovaya documents real and accumulating elite dissatisfaction under war strain, but explicitly notes that coup likelihood remains low because Russian elites are atomised and lack the interpersonal trust required for collective action. Her position — "mounting pressure but far from collapse" — is not a cascade-collapse thesis but a careful empirical assessment that, as the rebuttal below argues, NERAI's quantitative architecture in fact supports.

Rebuttal. Kendall-Taylor's cascade thesis identifies genuine structural constraints, but the NERAI data do not yet show cascade-phase elite breakdown. The argument depends on resource constraints being binding on elite coordination — yet the 12-month Military Escalation forecast (0.190, essentially flat from current 0.210) implies NERAI's model is pricing in continued military spending at current intensity, not resource-constraint-forced demobilisation. Second, NERAI's Political Instability bilateral index (0.080) shows no spike in Russia's external-alignment signalling that would indicate younger oligarchs or military commanders seeking external support or negotiated settlement independent of Kremlin-faction coordination. Third, the oligarch wealth decline, while real, has not yet forced visible defection: the Kremlin has adjusted factional compensation through security priority rather than wealth redistribution. Moderate Confidence the NERAI data are most consistent with managed internal repositioning under external securitisation, not autonomous cascade-phase collapse. Kendall-Taylor may well be correct that medium-term (18–36 month) structural constraints will eventually force elite reckoning; but the 12-month forecast window shows NERAI predicting stabilisation, not breakdown.

60-Day Watch List — NERAI Index Thresholds (19 May → 18 July 2026)

Eight monitorable triggers whose resolution will shift the scenario-probability allocation:

  • Coup Index trajectory (weekly) — 50–55/100 through June maintains base case; spike above 60 sustained signals stress case; fall below 48 by late June signals upside case.
  • Siloviki personnel moves (monthly) — 0–1 additional high-ranking removals (Lt. Gen or above, FSO/GRU/FSB/Rosgvardia) maintains base case; 2+ removals by August signal stress case; high-profile oligarch prosecution or exile signals upside case.
  • Military Escalation forecast revision — current 12-month projection 0.190. Upward revision in subsequent NERAI cycles signals stress case; decline below 0.180 favours upside case.
  • Political Instability bilateral index (weekly) — index crossing 0.15 signals cascade-phase risk; index declining below 0.060 by July signals upside case.
  • Bilateral Tension Index — current US-Russia tension at 46.9 (7-day trend improving). Reversion above 55 signals external securitisation failing to stabilise elite coordination; sustained decline below 45 signals managed securitisation succeeding.
  • FSO and military resource allocation (monthly) — visible expansion beyond current scope signals rapid consolidation; consolidation without public announcement signals managed securitisation; reversal of expansion would be anomalous and signal regime stress.
  • Commodity anchors — Brent above $120/bbl sustains external-risk premium and resource flows for factional compensation. Wheat above $680/bushel indicates food-security concerns. Equity-market loss of confidence in US-Russia settlement capacity would force Kremlin to reassess.
  • Ukraine military dynamics (weekly cross-check) — major Ukrainian territorial gains or Russian withdrawal from key sectors undermines the external-securitisation rationale and triggers elite reassessment (stress-case risk). Continued stalemate or Russian incremental advances maintain the securitisation equilibrium.

Methodological Caveat

The current NERAI Coup Index elevation reflects genuine signal movement, but the underlying data-generation process merits explicit transparency. The index aggregates GDELT event-data clustering, structured analyst judgment, and proprietary NERAI sentiment analysis on Russian-language sources. A high-salience disclosure event of the type referenced in the full brief would naturally trigger increased GDELT frequency and elevated sentiment-analysis scores on succession-related terms. A portion of the current near-term elevation may reflect news-cycle amplification.

However, the concurrent rise in corroborating non-news institutional events within a 60-day window suggests the NERAI spike captures genuine elite-repositioning activity, not merely media artifact. The 12-month forecast decline (−36%) provides further calibration: if the spike were pure news-cycle noise, the forecast would show no material decline because underlying drivers would be unchanged. The forecast decline suggests the model is interpreting the near-term spike as acute response combined with institutional counter-response, with both dynamics expected to stabilise the regime by Q2 2027. This interpretation carries Moderate Confidence — alternative readings (Kendall-Taylor cascade interpretation) might interpret the same data as indicating that institutional responses are inadequate to address structural constraints.

The NERAI index data are reliable; the causal interpretation of that signal (regime-destabilising vs. managed-repositioning) remains analytically contested. Readers should treat the 35%/45%/20% scenario allocation as ordinal rankings, not precise point estimates.
