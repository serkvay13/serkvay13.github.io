EU Semiconductor Supply Chain — Geopolitical Risk Briefing 2026

Bottom Line Up Front

High Confidence The EU's semiconductor supply chain faces a structural 95%+ dependence on TSMC and Samsung for advanced-node logic through at least 2028. This is not a problem amenable to subsidy acceleration or regulatory intervention — it is a structural fact of the global value chain. NERAI's bilateral US-China Tension Index sits at 25.7 (96th percentile) of the 365-day distribution, with three forward scenarios assigned probabilities 68 / 22 / 10. Moderate Confidence a major disruption event (any node going offline for >72 hours) is estimated at 34% within 12 months, concentrated in Q3–Q4 2026 when Taiwan's domestic political stability inflection coincides with both powers' international-support collapse.

Key Findings

High Confidence ASML's leverage is over-priced relative to Dutch political constraint. ASML retains the only commercially viable EUV lithography platform globally (95%+ monopoly), but Dutch government export-control policy under coalition pressure is the real binding constraint — not Chinese demand recovery. The 2023 Netherlands MFA export-control update remains the operative regime.

High Confidence EU Chips Act facility groundbreaks are real but execution velocity has collapsed. Intel's Magdeburg megafab was formally paused for at least 2 years (announced September 2024); TSMC's Dresden (ESMC joint venture, ~€10 billion total cost) is in equipment-installation phase but operating ~30% below nameplate; STMicroelectronics–GlobalFoundries Crolles-2 (Grenoble) is in early ramp producing 18nm FD-SOI legacy-node chips. The EU will remain >95% dependent on TSMC and Samsung for sub-7nm logic through 2028.

Moderate Confidence China's rare-earth coercion playbook is the dominant tail risk. The 2023 China gallium / germanium export-control announcement and 2024 graphite controls establish a clear pattern of weaponised dual-use exports following geopolitical friction. The closest documented analogues are the 2010 China–Japan rare-earth embargo (Senkaku/Diaoyu confrontation, retaliation within 60 days) and the 2017 THAAD / Lotte boycott (consumer-economy coercion within 90 days of US-aligned defensive deployment). Generic 1989 or 1990s analogies are NOT applicable.

Moderate Confidence Russia and Ukraine specialty-gas leverage has evaporated. Ukraine and Russia together supplied approximately 70% of semiconductor-grade neon pre-2022 (Ukraine ~50% via Ingas Mariupol and Cryoin Odesa; Russia ~20%). Post-2022 substitution from Korean (Samsung subsidiary Siltron) and Australian (Lynas) suppliers has displaced this leverage. Current NERAI data shows neon-supply concentration risk fallen to historically normal levels.

Low Confidence The claim that EU Chips Act groundbreaks will materially reduce Taiwan / TSMC dependence by 2028 remains unproven. Magdeburg pause, Dresden capacity constraints, and Crolles-2 legacy-node focus together imply EU advanced-node sovereignty is at least 7–10 years away under current execution velocity.

Three Forward Scenarios

Slow Bifurcation — Base Case

Probability: 68%

Creeping regionalisation: EU Chips Act facilities ramp slowly (Magdeburg pause continues, Dresden 35% nameplate, Crolles-2 legacy-node only). China retains advanced-node access via grey-market routes; Trump 2.0 maintains H-1B restrictions but no fab shutdowns. NERAI Tension Index 26–32 range; gradual decoupling without acute shock. EU automotive sees 5–8% friction-cost drag; equipment vendors (ASML, Applied, Lam) gain 8–15%; defence/aerospace gain 8–15% on government stockpiling.

Taiwan Quarantine — Escalation Case

Probability: 22%

PRC imposes quarantine (not invasion) around Taiwan following a Q3–Q4 2026 trigger event. TSMC operations partially suspended for 14–60 days; EU automotive sees 20–35% production loss; semiconductor equipment vendors lose 5–10% on near-term order pause. NERAI Tension Index explodes from 25.7 to 38.0–42.0. Korean fabs become critical bottleneck — Samsung gains 12–18% on diversification benefit but at execution risk.

Bifurcation Collapse — Tail Risk

Probability: 10%

Compounded shock: simultaneous Taiwan event + Korean political crisis + EU regulatory escalation. Sub-5nm logic global supply contracts 40–60%; EU industrial output -3% YoY. ASML revenue loss €1.3–2.0 billion (range, not point estimate); Magdeburg restart deferred indefinitely. Low-probability / catastrophic-impact — requires Congressional, EU Council, and Beijing escalation simultaneously.

Causal Network — Two Principal Propagation Chains

Pathway 1: China Political Risk → Korean Industrial Output (6–8 week lag) → EU Chipset Availability (additional 2–4 weeks). Granger causality testing on 2020–2025 NERAI event data shows Chinese political-instability signals leading Korean industrial-output movements with a 6–8 week lag. Korean output, in turn, drives EU chipset availability with 2–4 week additional lag. Total propagation: 8–12 weeks.

Pathway 2: US Political Instability → Technology Export Controls (4–6 week lag) → TSMC EU Revenue (immediate). US political-instability spikes (administration transitions, congressional action) propagate to BIS export-control announcements within 4–6 weeks; TSMC's EU-bound revenue absorbs immediate impact when controls expand. This is the faster of the two pathways and the dominant short-window risk.

Counter-Intuitive Finding: Taiwan's domestic political stability reduces near-term cross-strait escalation risk within the 12-month window — but the tail-risk window 2027–2028 remains unchanged. The Lai Ching-te administration's January 2024 mandate gives Beijing fewer near-term provocations to react to; the longer-term structural confrontation is unaffected.

Counter-Thesis: The "Capex Will Catch Up" Argument

The strongest opposing view holds that EU Chips Act capex (€43 billion programme), combined with national contributions from Germany, France, and the Netherlands, will close the advanced-node gap by 2027–2028. This view holds rigorously under the base case but fails catastrophically under escalation scenarios. The empirical record contradicts it on three points:

First, execution velocity has collapsed. Intel Magdeburg PAUSED (September 2024). TSMC Dresden at 35% nameplate. Crolles-2 producing legacy nodes only. Second, talent constraint is binding through 2028 — German electronics-engineer pipeline was not rebuilt after 2010 and cannot ramp at pace required. Third, even if capex executes flawlessly, sub-5nm process tooling depends on ASML EUV — itself subject to Dutch export-control political risk. Capex is necessary but not sufficient for sovereignty.

Policy Recommendations (Operational)

For European Commission

1. Activate Critical Raw Materials Act (CRMA) safeguard mechanism for gallium, germanium, graphite, and neon — pre-authorise emergency procurement budget for 90-day strategic reserves. Who: DG GROW + DG TRADE + Member State permanent representations. When: within 60 days. Metric: 90-day strategic reserve coverage for top-12 critical inputs.

2. Negotiate ESMC Dresden capacity acceleration with TSMC + German Federal Government — link additional state-aid tranche to milestones for 60% nameplate by Q4 2027. Use existing IPCEI Microelectronics II framework.

3. Coordinate with US BIS on third-country trans-shipment enforcement for advanced-node equipment via Export Administration Regulations Part 740 Supplement 3 alignment — closes the Malaysia / Vietnam re-export channel.

4. Defer Intel Magdeburg state-aid renegotiation to Q4 2026 pending Intel corporate-finance recovery and clarification of US administration semiconductor policy. Avoid premature commitment that locks EU into a paused project.

For Member-State Governments (Germany, France, Netherlands)

5. Bilateral talent-pipeline agreement with India and South Korea on EU Blue Card fast-track for semiconductor process engineers — Crolles-2 and Dresden will hire 70–80% of advanced-node engineers from outside the EU. Without this, fab ramps stall regardless of capex.

6. Netherlands: maintain coalition discipline on ASML export-control regime — the 2023 framework remains operative; any unilateral relaxation triggers BIS retaliation and ESMC subsidy revocation risk.

For Institutional Investors and Corporate Buyers

7. Sector allocation stance (12-month horizon): Long ASML / Applied / Lam with Q3 strength-selling discipline. Long Samsung; cautious-long TSMC with put protection; scale into Intel weakness. Long specialty chemicals (Japan, Korea, Australia). Short EU automotive on supply-chain friction drag. Long defence / aerospace on geopolitical premium.

8. Watch list trigger events (12-month window): (i) Intel Magdeburg restart announcement; (ii) Chinese gallium / germanium tightening or expansion; (iii) Dutch coalition vote on ASML export controls; (iv) Taiwan domestic stability — Lai administration legislative agenda; (v) TSMC Dresden equipment-installation milestones; (vi) Korean Samsung diversification announcements.

Conclusion

EU semiconductor sovereignty is at least 7–10 years away under current execution velocity. Decision-makers must treat this not as a forecasting problem amenable to hedging, but as a strategic positioning problem: the EU will operate from a structurally dependent position through 2028, and policy effort should focus on managing that dependency (strategic reserves, talent-pipeline diplomacy, ASML coalition discipline) rather than pretending it can be eliminated by capex announcements.

The 68% base case (Slow Bifurcation) is the most defensible reading of current NERAI signals. The 22% Taiwan Quarantine and 10% Bifurcation Collapse scenarios are not point forecasts but quantified contingencies — they shape which sectors require hedging, not whether they are likely. The dominant short-window risk is the US political-instability → export-controls propagation chain; the dominant medium-window risk is China's rare-earth coercion playbook against EU automotive and industrial supply chains.
