Non-Forecast Notice. The scenarios and probability values in this brief are conditional analytical inferences about pre-electoral conditions in The Gambia — not electoral forecasts or predictions of vote outcomes. Vote-share ranges are illustrative conditional indications, not predictions of how Gambian voters will cast their ballots. The Gambia's electoral outcome will be determined by Gambian voters through the Independent Electoral Commission's processes. This report takes no position on, and offers no inference about, the choices any Gambian voter or candidate will or should make. Nothing here constitutes financial, legal, or investment advice.

Bottom Line Up Front

High Confidence The Gambia is exhibiting a genuine pre-electoral instability accumulation in the 6 months leading to the late-2026 presidential election. NERAI's 15 May 2026 signal cluster places four core stability indices simultaneously at the 98th–99.4th percentile of the 365-day distribution — a recognisable pre-electoral squeeze signature consistent with Pattern P5 leadership-transition-window dynamics (match score 0.67).

The election is not at imminent risk of violent disruption, but the political temperature is rising faster than the mainstream news cycle reflects, and Senegal's PASTEF government under President Bassirou Diomaye Faye will be the critical external arbiter of whether Barrow's institutional consolidation triggers cross-border destabilisation or measured external mediation.

Key Findings

High Confidence The NERAI cluster is a quantitative surfacing of institutional dynamics, not a statistical anomaly. The co-elevation of ethnic-religious violence, political repression and institutional deterioration at the 99.4th percentile maps precisely onto three observable drivers: (i) TRRC implementation friction — ongoing Special Prosecutor's Office cases against 15–20 Jammeh-era security figures, with verdicts now issuing at 2–3 per month; (ii) NPP–APRC alliance management strain, where retaining Jola-ethnic APRC voters while consolidating Mandinka-Fulani Barrow supporters creates factional pressure inside the executive and security apparatus; (iii) opposition coalition formation, which forces the security apparatus to increase monitoring and restrict public-assembly permits — registering as repression in event-data systems.

High Confidence The "ethnic-religious violence" spike is institutional contestation, not mass communal rioting. The signal captures mosque-sermon disputes over Islamic Council alignment, ethnically-tinged market conflicts in Serekunda, regional resource-scarcity tensions in Foni and Upper River, and the Sufi–Salafi institutional schism. These acquire political salience in pre-electoral periods and aggregate into observable event data well before they reach the headline-grabbing threshold.

Moderate Confidence Opposition mobilisation is intensifying but has not yet crossed into systematic suppression. UDP rallies of 2,000–3,000 (Serekunda, March 2026), AVSHV/TRRC waterfront demonstrations (Banjul, April 2026), and joint UDP-GDC mobilisation in the West Coast Region (May 2026) have been monitored but not dispersed. Internet throttling and broadcast-licence revocations have not been reported — distinguishing The Gambia from more repressive Sahel-region patterns (Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger).

Moderate Confidence Senegal's PASTEF government is the single most consequential external variable. President Faye's pan-African sovereignty posture contrasts sharply with Macky Sall's institutional-partnership orientation. PASTEF will likely issue public criticism of any pre-electoral squeeze escalation but will not deploy military force without explicit ECOWAS Commission authorisation. Senegalese leverage is primarily diplomatic and economic (transit tariffs, cross-border commerce, water-sharing in the Senegambia basin), not military.

Moderate Confidence Opposition coalition unification by June 30 is the highest-impact directional indicator. If UDP (Darboe), GDC (Kandeh), PDOIS (Sallah) and C4C (Fatty) announce a joint candidacy framework, the stress-case probability rises materially. As of mid-May 2026, Track-2 talks via civil-society mediators are active but no unity-candidate framework has been published.

Low Confidence The "everything is fine" counter-thesis is internally coherent but reads the NERAI signal backwards. The argument that mainstream-news absence invalidates the signal mistakes early-warning value for noise: NERAI's value proposition is precisely to surface pre-electoral accumulation before the mainstream news cycle catches up. The signal is in the pre-event accumulation phase — exactly where decision-support is most valuable.

Three Forward Scenarios

Scenario 1 — Peaceful Incumbent Retention (Base Case)

Analytical Likelihood: ~65%

Opposition coalition remains fragmented through October 2026; Barrow retains office in an indicative 50–55% vote-share band (conditional, not a forecast). Minor irregularities, no systematic fraud, constitutional disputes resolved within 60–90 days. International observers note "credible competition despite isolated irregularities." NERAI trajectory: ethnic-religious violence moderates to z=2.5–3.0; political repression remains elevated then drops sharply post-election. Currency stability within 2–3%, Chinese infrastructure projects uninterrupted, ECOMIG modest drawdown.

Scenario 2 — Contested Incumbent Retention (Stress Case)

Analytical Likelihood: ~25%

UDP-led coalition consolidates 40–45% on a unity candidate; Barrow margin narrows to 48–52% (conditional indicative range). Ballot-box stuffing in 10–15% of stations triggers constitutional challenges and post-election street mobilisation (500–2,000 participants per city). Police escalate to water cannon / tear gas in 2–3 incidents. Supreme Court sustains result by mid-January 2027; opposition rejects ruling, commits to "continuous accountability demands." Dalasi depreciates 5–8% then recovers; ECOWAS issues mediation proposal; AU PSC convenes emergency session; donor programming tranches delayed 3–6 months.

Scenario 3 — Opposition Breakthrough or Election Disruption (Tail Case)

Analytical Likelihood: ~10%

UDP-led unified opposition secures 45–50% (conditional). Either: deadlocked Supreme Court, GAF Jola-faction intervention citing electoral illegality, or "security-concerns" postponement. NERAI ethnic-religious violence z≥4.5–5.0; political repression z≥4.5 including opposition-leader detention; potential coup-index spike. Dalasi -15 to -25% indicative; banking deposit runs; donor disbursements frozen; ECOMIG reinforcement; possible ICC referral if atrocities occur. Senegalese border-troop precaution; remittances decline 30–50% (analytical projection conditional on the scenario, not investment guidance).

External Actors & Their Gambian Stakes

Senegal (PASTEF / Faye government) — The single most consequential external variable. Three competing interests: Casamance ceasefire durability, West African hegemonic positioning post-Sall, and transit-trade dependency through Banjul. Likely posture: neutral monitor, no military force without ECOWAS authorisation, public criticism if pre-electoral squeeze escalates.

ECOWAS (Commission & ECOMIG) — Critical decision point on whether to extend, draw-down, or re-terminate ECOMIG (~700–800 troops, Yundum airport since 2017). Will deploy an election observation mission but will not pre-position ECOMIG reinforcements unless Banjul explicitly requests them. The 2022 coup-attempt prosecution precedent constrains the legal basis for intervention absent clear democratic illegality.

European Union — Largest bilateral donor (€450M multi-year envelope 2023–2027). Three drivers: election support, migration management (Gambia is a top-three irregular West African source country), development-aid leverage. Will field a robust 40–50 person observation mission; quietly co-fund opposition civil-society participation; tolerate "managed elections" provided UDP contestation is accepted.

United States (MCC, AFRICOM, State) — MCC compact suspended since 2019; un-reactivated as of May 2026, signalling lingering State Department doubts. AFRICOM coordinates with GAF on professionalisation; minimal physical presence in-country. Leverage is modest — neither military nor financial coercion is credible at scale.

China — Strict non-interventionist posture, ~$80–100M in outstanding development loans, Banjul–Barra bridge, port modernisation, Huawei telecoms contracts. Visibly neutral, quietly cultivating relationships with opposition figures.

Turkey (TIKA / Diyanet) — Hospital, school and seminary partnerships position Turkey as a non-colonial alternative to Gulf states; cultural-geopolitical rather than directly electoral interest.

Equatorial Guinea (Obiang) — Hosts Yahya Jammeh in long-term exile under 2017 ECOWAS-mediated arrangement. The exile remains a structural variable for any future TRRC extradition or in-absentia prosecution decision.

Russia / Africa Corps — Minimal current presence relative to Mali / Burkina Faso / Niger; monitoring The Gambia as a contingent foothold if ECOMIG withdraws and GAF seeks external partnerships. Low-probability pathway as of mid-2026.

60-Day Watch List — Mid-May to Mid-July 2026

Ten monitorable triggers, ordered by directional impact on scenario likelihoods. Each updates the base / stress / tail allocation as it resolves:

  • Barrow's third-term candidacy announcement by 15 June — formal declaration favours base case (+5%); delay beyond 1 July raises tail-case probability +3–5%.
  • Opposition coalition unification (UDP / GDC / PDOIS / C4C) by 30 June — joint candidacy framework shifts stress case +8%, tail case +2%.
  • TRRC verdict on a high-profile Jammeh-era figure — acquittal or politically-perceived conviction activates victim-advocacy mobilisation; stress-case +2 to +3%.
  • GAF court-martial sentencing on remaining 2022 coup-attempt defendants — sentences <10 years signal officer-corps sympathy; >15 years signal factional rift.
  • Senegal / PASTEF policy statement on The Gambia's election — neutrality favours base case; opposition-support hints push stress / tail +5% combined.
  • Opposition rally restriction or police use of force against a >1,000-person rally — political-repression index spike +0.5; stress case +5%, tail case +2%.
  • NERAI ethnic-religious violence threshold — z > 4.5 by end of June moves stress / tail combined +5%; moderation to z < 3.5 favours base case +3%.
  • Diaspora remittance flow shift — 5%+ decline in June–July via mobile-money platforms favours stress case +2%.
  • EU / US election observation mission deployment timing — early deployment (before August) favours base case; delay favours stress case +3%.
  • AFRICOM / UK Defence engagement with GAF on election-period neutrality — absence of such engagement by 15 July is concerning; tail case +2%.

Counter-Thesis: "Everything Is Fine"

The strongest opposing reading rests on five claims: institutional continuity since the failed 2022 coup attempt; democratic norms held through four post-2016 electoral cycles; economic stabilisation and durable diaspora remittances ($250–300M/yr); Senegalese stability and PASTEF's low-intervention appetite; and the argument that the NERAI elevation is methodological noise absent mainstream-press corroboration.

Moderate-to-High Confidence The counter-thesis is internally coherent but reads the signal backwards. The mechanisms of Barrow's consolidation — court-martial of officers, APRC alliance formation, Mandinka-voter erosion — are themselves the pre-electoral strain generating instability in hybrid democracies. Institutional stability and pre-electoral institutional tightening are not mutually exclusive. The 2021 result was decisive at 53%; a narrower 2026 outcome would face a fundamentally different judicial context, with heightened TRRC sensitisation and more organised opposition coalitions. The "NERAI is noise" claim is logically inverted: a 99.4th-percentile elevation across three indices, plus a 0.67 Pattern P5 match, is precisely the leading-indicator state at which early-warning systems are most valuable. The base case (~65%) remains most likely — but the stress-case pathway (25%) is substantially more plausible than the "everything is fine" narrative acknowledges.

Policy Recommendations (Operational)

For Institutional Investors

1. Implement dalasi hedging by 30 June 2026. Establish 20–30% downside-protection via USD/GMD currency-forwards or options for equity positions in Gambian telecoms, banking and infrastructure. Unwind trigger: post-election verdict acceptance by opposition.

2. Stress-test sovereign credit-worthiness under a scenario where tax revenue declines 10–15% post-election. Engage with IMF Gambia desk and WAEMU on contingency planning given the 80–85% debt-to-GDP starting point.

3. Defer major infrastructure-tender participation until post-election clarity (October 2026+) to avoid invalidation / renegotiation risk under a new government.

For Donor Agencies (EU, World Bank, USAID, IMF, AfDB)

4. Activate a pre-electoral Track-1.5 governance dialogue via the EU Delegation in Dakar / World Bank Gambia country office. Target: 2–3 facilitated sessions between 15 June and 30 August 2026; joint government–opposition communique on constitutional dispute resolution as success metric.

5. Establish contingency disbursement protocols keyed to NERAI political-instability sustained above z=3.0 and observation-mission credibility assessment — not unilateral donor judgment.

6. Fund independent parallel observation ($500K–$1M to IFES / KAS / NERAI) to issue preliminary credibility assessment within 72 hours of polling.

For ECOWAS

7. Authorise a pre-electoral mediation mission (sitting ECOWAS Commission Vice President or external mediator from South Africa / Kenya) in July–August 2026; separate consultations with Barrow, Darboe and other opposition leaders on dispute-resolution sequencing.

8. Extend the ECOMIG mandate through December 2026 with an explicit election-security role. Pause current drawdown discussions; preserve the 700–800 troop ceiling; conduct a joint election-security planning exercise with the GAF demonstrating neutral posture.

For Senegal (PASTEF)

Establish bilateral observer coordination with the IEC (15–25 Senegalese technical observers embedded in ECOWAS framework); pre-position quiet border-monitoring capacity in Casamance and Kolda for refugee / militia detection; issue 1–2 scrupulously neutral public statements (June and September 2026) reaffirming respect for the Gambian process.

For the United States and United Kingdom

Joint / harmonised State Department + FCDO messaging in August–September on credibility and dispute resolution; discrete AFRICOM and UK Defence Ministry conversations with GAF leadership on election-period neutrality by 31 July; privately signal that a peaceful, credible election unlocks MCC compact-resumption discussions in 2027 — and that fraud or repression forecloses them.

Conclusion

The Gambia is not the next Sahel. Mali, Burkina Faso and Niger collapsed through coercive military pathways under different external partnerships and far weaker institutional buffers. The Gambia's 2026 election is the cleaner case: a hybrid democracy in pre-electoral tightening mode, where the incumbent retains durable control of security forces, the security apparatus is consolidating loyalty through court proceedings rather than open violence, and the opposition is mobilising at measurable intensity without yet crossing into systematic suppression.

The base case — peaceful incumbent retention at ~65% analytical likelihood — is the most defensible reading of current NERAI signals. The 25% stress case and 10% tail case are not point forecasts but quantified contingencies: they shape which hedges, observation commitments and mediation protocols are needed, not whether they are likely. The dominant short-window risk is opposition rally policing escalating into a use-of-force incident; the dominant medium-window risk is a unified-opposition consolidation that narrows Barrow's margin enough to trigger legal contestation under heightened TRRC-era scrutiny. Decision-makers operating exposure across The Gambia — donor portfolios, infrastructure tenders, dalasi positions, observation deployments — should treat the 60-day watch list as the operative dashboard between now and mid-July 2026.
